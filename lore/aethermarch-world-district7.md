# CHRONICLES OF AETHERMARCH
## World Document — Lower District 7
### "The Place That Kept Going"
### Version 1.0

---

# OVERVIEW

District 7 is the lowest inhabited district in Aethermarch's vertical hierarchy — the furthest from the golden light at the top of the tower, the deepest in the smog, the first place the collapse hit and the last place anyone came to help.

It is also, against every odd, alive.

Not the curated, maintained, updated life of the upper districts. Something rawer than that. Something that wasn't handed to anyone. The kind of life that forms when people refuse to stop living even after everything tells them to.

District 7 is two things at once and the tension between them is what makes it feel real:

**The skeleton is frozen.** The architecture, the systems, the infrastructure — all of it stuck at the moment of the collapse like software that never got its final update. Beautiful, intricate, stagnant.

**The people are not.** They animated the frozen skeleton with something the utopia never had — pure, unpolished, unmanufactured resilience. Every beautiful thing in District 7 was made from nothing, from scraps, from the bones of a world that left them behind.

---

# THE ARCHITECTURE

## What It Was
District 7 was built during the height of the utopia period — and it shows. Even now, even through two years of abandonment, the bones of the district are extraordinary. Aethermarch believed in beauty for everyone during the utopia. The architecture of District 7 reflects that.

Buildings of dark stone and pale magitek alloy, intricately detailed — carved cornices, ornate pipe work, aether conduit housings designed to be looked at as much as used. Plazas with geometric tile work. Street lamps with delicate brass fittings. Doorways with magitek sensor arrays so elegant they looked like jewelry. Everything built with the assumption that the people living here deserved craftsmanship.

That assumption turned out to be temporary.

## What It Is Now
The buildings are still standing. Still structurally sound for the most part. Still showing every detail of what they were designed to be.

But frozen.

Not biological decay — not rot, not collapse, not nature reclaiming anything. **Software decay.** The specific stillness of a system still running on hardware nobody is maintaining anymore. Still doing what it was told to do. Just... stuck. Stagnant. While the upper districts kept updating, kept pressing forward, District 7 simply stopped at the moment of the collapse and has been running the same loop ever since.

**What software decay looks like in stone and metal:**

The aether conduits beneath the cobblestones still pulse. But the color is wrong — not the warm gold of the utopia, not the clean blue of active Aethon systems. Something in between. Something that hasn't been calibrated in two years. The glow flickers in patterns that almost make sense, like a loading screen that never quite resolves.

Automated doors still open and close. For nobody. On the schedule they were given two years ago, faithfully maintained by systems that don't know the people they were built for are gone.

Building directories still list businesses that closed the day of the collapse. The little illuminated signs still cycle through their menus. IRONHAVEN FRESH MARKET — LEVEL 2. AETHON COMMUNITY WELLNESS CENTER — OPEN 8AM-8PM. DR. YEVA CALIN, MAGITEK MEDICINE — BY APPOINTMENT.

The appointments are two years overdue.

Street lighting still follows its old timer — bright at dusk, dimming toward midnight, brightening again at dawn. The rhythm of a city that believed in itself. The lights don't know the city changed.

Aethon advertisement displays, mounted on the sides of buildings at regular intervals, still cycle their content. The same three advertisements on rotation, day after day, two years running. A family laughing in a clean bright kitchen. A child holding an AI companion. The tagline: *"Building Tomorrow, Together."*

Nobody has updated the displays.

Nobody has turned them off.

## The Beauty Underneath
Here is the thing about software decay that distinguishes it from ruin: **the beauty is still there.** Underneath the stagnation, underneath the grime that two years of smog has settled into every carved surface, the architecture of District 7 is still extraordinary.

On a clear morning — rare, but they happen — when the smog thins and the light comes through at the right angle, you can see it. The way the buildings were designed to catch that light. The way the pipe work traces patterns that were meant to be looked at. The way the whole district was laid out with a logic that assumed the people living in it mattered.

It still looks like that. Just dusty. Just still. Just running the same loop.

The elites kept updating. District 7 just stopped.

# THE SENSORY EXPERIENCE

## What You Smell
Rusted metal — not the clean iron smell of a working forge but something older, something that's been sitting in damp air too long. Underneath that: aether fumes. Not the clean energy smell of maintained conduits but something corrupted, something that's been cycling through cracked pipes for two years without maintenance. Toxic in the specific way of things that were never meant to leak.

Underneath everything else, occasionally, cutting through when the wind shifts: something cooking. Something that smells incredible. Life insisting on itself.

## What You Hear
The district is never quiet. But its noise is layered — two distinct soundscapes running simultaneously.

**The background — the frozen skeleton:**
Automated systems cycling. Doors opening and closing. The aether conduits humming their slightly-wrong hum. The distant rotation of Syndicate monitoring towers. The occasional clank of infrastructure under pressure it wasn't designed to sustain forever. The ghost sounds of a system still doing its job even though the job is over.

**The foreground — the living people:**
Arguments spilling out of windows. Children in the street below, using abandoned magitek housings as climbing structures, their shouts echoing off stone walls. Someone three floors up playing an instrument — something stringed, something old, something that doesn't require power. Market stalls in the old plaza, vendors calling out in the particular shorthand of people who've been doing this long enough to have developed their own language for it. The crackle of black market comms units. Somewhere, always, a generator struggling.

The contrast between these two layers is District 7's defining sound. The frozen hum of what was underneath the alive noise of what is.

## What You See
Outdated technology everywhere — not garbage, archaeology. These are artifacts of the utopia. AI companion units with cracked shells, their lights long dead, repurposed as storage containers or planters or just left where they fell. Aethon branded products half buried in rubble, their logos still crisp, still legible, still cheerful. Magitek fixtures that used to do something specific and beautiful, now doing nothing, now serving as anchor points for laundry lines strung between buildings.

The Syndicate monitoring towers at regular intervals — automated, no human operators, their sensor arrays sweeping slow red arcs across the streets on their predetermined schedules. The people of District 7 know these schedules the way farmers know weather. They move around them without thinking about it.

And above everything, just barely visible through the smog on most days: the faintest suggestion of gold. The upper districts. The maintained utopia. Close enough to see the idea of. Far enough to be another world entirely.

## What You Feel
The cobblestones are uneven — two years of no maintenance, frost and heat and the occasional Hex Wraith incident taking their toll. You learn to watch your feet in District 7. The air is heavy. Not just the smog — something in the aether contamination gives the air a weight it shouldn't have, a thickness you feel in your chest on bad days. On good days it's just the smog.

The particular texture of a community that made itself out of necessity. The way people move through shared space when they've been sharing it under pressure for two years — efficiently, aware of each other, a choreography nobody choreographed.

# THE PEOPLE WHO STAYED

Not everyone left when the quarantine went up. Some couldn't — no resources, no connections in the upper districts, nowhere to go. Some refused — this was their home, their community, their wall of names, and they weren't going to let Aethon's quarantine notice tell them otherwise.

They are, without exception, extraordinary.

Not in a romantic way. Not in the way stories sometimes make poor people into symbols. In the specific, unglamorous, daily way of people who woke up every morning in impossible circumstances and just kept going because the alternative was worse.

## Who They Are

**The ones who stayed by choice:** Older residents mostly. People whose whole lives were built in District 7 — whose businesses, whose relationships, whose identities were so bound up in this specific place that leaving felt like dying anyway. They tend the wall of names. They maintain the informal networks of mutual aid that keep the district functioning. They know everyone. They remember what it was.

**The ones who stayed by necessity:** Younger, mostly. People without the resources or connections to get out when it mattered. Vyx's demographic. They've adapted fastest, built the black market economy, learned the patrol schedules, developed the skills to survive in the gap between what the Syndicate allows and what people actually need. They tend not to talk about the before. They're focused on the now.

**The ones who came back:** A smaller group. People who got out, made it to the mid-districts or higher, and came back anyway. For family. For community. For reasons they don't always explain. These are the ones District 7 talks about in a particular tone — part admiration, part confusion, part something that might be love.

## The Resilience

Every beautiful thing in District 7 was made from nothing.

The wall of names — someone decided the disappeared deserved to be remembered even after the official records stopped caring. Someone got paint. Someone started writing. Others added to it. It grew.

The market in the old plaza — someone set up the first stall in the first week of the quarantine, in the shell of what used to be a Syndicate-approved commercial district, and just started trading. Others followed. Now it's the economic heart of the district, running entirely outside Aethon's systems, operating on trust and reputation and the particular ruthlessness of people who have no margin for error.

The music on the corner — someone decided beauty still mattered here. Especially here.

The pot Mrs. Orveil lent Vyx — someone decided that's what neighbors do, even now. Especially now.

This is what the Syndicate's reports mean when they write "low social capital" and "minimal civic footprint." They mean a community they couldn't quantify. Couldn't monetize. Couldn't control.

They mean this.

# THE BLACK MARKET

District 7's unofficial economy is older than the collapse — it existed in the shadows of the utopia too, serving the people the official economy didn't reach. But the collapse formalized it. Made it the only economy that mattered.

**What gets traded:**
- Salvaged magitek components — the primary commodity. Everything broken in District 7 can be stripped for parts that still work. Vyx's entire livelihood.
- Food — the Syndicate's official supply lines to District 7 are technically operational. The quantities they deliver are technically sufficient. Neither of these things means people aren't hungry.
- Information — patrol schedules, Syndicate movement patterns, which monitoring towers have blind spots, which agents can be bribed. Knowledge as currency.
- Medicine — the Aethon Community Wellness Center is still listed in the building directory. It has been closed for two years. People get sick anyway.
- Aether — raw, unprocessed, dangerous. Siphoned from the conduits beneath the streets by people who know exactly how much risk they're taking and have decided it's worth it.

**How it works:**
No fixed location — the market moves. The plaza is the open-air face of it, the relatively safe stuff, the things that don't technically violate quarantine regulations enough for the Syndicate to bother about. The real market is behind that. Accessed through relationships, through reputation, through being known by the right people.

Vyx knows the right people. He's been selling salvage here for two years.

# THE HEX WRAITH PROBLEM

Since the collapse, Hex Wraiths have been appearing in District 7 with increasing frequency. The Syndicate's official explanation is aetheric contamination — a natural byproduct of the damaged conduit network. The Syndicate's actual knowledge is considerably more specific than that.

The Wraiths are drawn to aetheric energy sources — the conduits beneath the streets, the salvaged components in Vyx's bag, the black market aether trade. They move at night primarily, though "primarily" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The safe hours are approximately dawn to two hours before dusk. After that, you move carefully or you don't move.

The monitoring towers track Wraith activity — officially to "contain the contamination," practically to make sure the creatures stay in the lower districts and don't drift upward toward more valuable real estate.

The people of District 7 have developed a working relationship with the danger. Not comfort — nobody is comfortable. But familiarity. They know the signs. They know the sounds. They know when to get inside and when the sound they're hearing is probably just the pipes.

Probably.

# KEY LOCATIONS IN DISTRICT 7

## The Substation Ruins (Scene 01)
A collapsed aetheric substation — one of several that have failed in District 7 since the collapse, the infrastructure slowly dying without maintenance. Rich scavenging territory. Dangerous structurally. Worth it if you know what you're doing.

Vyx knows what he's doing.

## The Wall of Names
On the eastern face of what used to be a community center — now gutted, windows empty, automated doors still faithfully opening and closing — someone started writing names. The people who disappeared into the quarantine, into Aethon's facilities, into the Hex Wraith incidents. The people the official records stopped tracking.

The wall is two months old. It is already longer than anyone expected it to get.

## The Plaza Market
The economic heart of District 7. Open-air, officially tolerated, practically essential. Runs from just after dawn patrol sweep to just before dusk. Everything from salvaged components to questionable food to information. The black market proper starts in the three buildings surrounding the plaza — accessed through specific doors, known to specific people.

## Vyx's Workshop
A converted storage unit on the ground floor of a half-collapsed residential building. Heavy door, basic lock (Pip's assessment: very basic). Inside: salvaged magitek on every shelf, a workbench covered in half-finished repairs, a cot in the corner that's more organized than it looks.

A place someone lives in carefully. A place that was home.

## The Monitoring Tower — Grid Point 7-4
The tower Vyx ducks away from on reflex during Scene 02. Sensor array sweep: 34-second rotation, 12-second blind spot on the northwest corner. Vyx has known this for eight months. He doesn't think about how he knows it anymore. He just knows it.

# DISTRICT 7 IN THE STORY

District 7 is not just a setting. It is a character.

It is what Vyx is fighting for — not abstractly, not symbolically, but specifically. These streets. These people. Mrs. Orveil's pot. The wall of names. The musician on the corner. The market in the plaza. The community that made itself out of nothing because the alternative was worse.

When Vyx leaves District 7 at the end of Scene 03, he looks back once. The player has spent three scenes in these streets. They know what he's looking at.

That look back costs something.

It should.
